Engineering organizations that scale past 200 people face a choice: add management layers and slow down, or build a culture where leaders develop leaders and teams self-organize. Spotify has always chosen the latter — and that's exactly the kind of culture I build.

In my most recent role, I unified a post-merger engineering organization of 250 engineers under a single leadership philosophy. The challenge wasn't technical — it was getting leaders to stop directing and start developing. I introduced a 6-point accountability clarity framework that gave every team clear ownership boundaries, then trained engineering managers to lead with questions instead of answers. The result: teams that solve problems before they escalate, without waiting for permission.

One story captures my approach. A brilliant engineer had been labeled "difficult" by previous management. I recognized the behavior came from not feeling heard. I created space for their ideas, gave them ownership of a challenging problem, and protected them from organizational politics. They became our highest performer and now mentor others. The problem was never the person — it was the environment.

What draws me to the VP of Engineering role at Spotify is the people-first engineering culture. My career arc — from broadcast engineering in Germany, to touring production for U2 and the Rolling Stones, to leading global engineering teams — taught me that the best technical outcomes come from empowered people, not prescribed processes. Spotify's squad model resonates deeply with how I lead.

I'd enjoy exploring how my experience building ownership cultures could contribute to Spotify's platform engineering organization.

Wolfgang Schram
