When a media company's engineering org is caught between legacy systems and a streaming-first future, the real challenge isn't technical migration — it's getting 300 engineers to own the transformation instead of waiting for someone to tell them what to do.

That's the problem I solve. At my previous company, I inherited a post-merger engineering organization of 250 engineers spread across four continents with zero unified leadership philosophy. I introduced a 6-point accountability clarity framework, trained leaders to lead with questions rather than directives, and gave regional leads full domain ownership. Within 18 months, teams were self-organizing — delivering without constant oversight, solving problems before they escalated.

I've also learned that the best engineering cultures are built one leader at a time. A promising engineer on my team was struggling to make the leap to leadership. Instead of status updates, I ran weekly coaching sessions — asking questions, letting them fail safely, then reflecting together. Today, they lead their own team and develop others the same way. That's the multiplier effect I build.

What excites me about the SVP Engineering role at Warner Bros Discovery is the intersection of broadcast heritage and streaming innovation. My own career followed a similar arc — from hands-on broadcast engineering in Germany, to designing touring production systems for U2 and the Rolling Stones, to leading global engineering teams. I understand what it means to operate where failure is visible to millions, and I know how to build the leadership culture that thrives under that pressure.

I'd welcome the chance to discuss how my experience building ownership cultures could help Max's engineering organization navigate its next chapter.
